ABSTRACT
Numerous machine-learning algorithms are employed predominantly for the classification 
of data presented in a tabular format, yet there has been very little research regarding their 
efficacy when presented with smaller amounts of data. The study explores performance comparisons 
between three of the more commonly applied algorithms, Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and 
XGBoost, across four different datasets; two healthcare and two finance data tables where sample 
sizes ranged from 303 to 30,000. Performance evaluation was conducted on the basis of accuracy, 
F1 score, and ROC-AUC with identical pre-processing and train-test split techniques used on all 
four datasets.
Model performance is dependent upon sample sizes as illustrated by the most significant difference 
in performance occurring with the smallest dataset, (Heart Disease, 303 samples; Logistic 
Regression 0.7635) indicating superior performance for Logistic Regression when compared to 
Random Forest (0.7647) and XGBoost (0.7515), while providing the best overall balanced performance. 
In larger datasets Random Forest consistently provided superior performance in three out of four 
datasets (German Credit, 0.8044; Insurance, 0.9481). XGBoost did not achieve the best performance 
on any of the four datasets. Additionally, in one of the datasets (Credit Card), performance was 
characterized by severe class imbalance with F1 scores less than 0.50 in spite of average accuracy 
greater than eighty percent.
We conclude that ensemble methods (e.g., Random Forest and XGBoost) run the risk of overfitting using 
limited dataset sizes (e.g., < 500) while generalizing more accurately when using simpler linear 
models. Therefore, our findings provide practical advice for practitioners on how to choose 
algorithms when using very small tables (like healthcare or finance).

INTRODUCTION

Machine learning models are widely used for classification tasks on tabular datasets, but their 
performance on small datasets is not well understood. We compare three popular methods—Logistic 
Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost—across four datasets from healthcare and finance with sample 
sizes ranging from 303 to 30,000. Our goal is to determine which models work best when training 
data is limited.

Random Forest achieved the best performance on three out of four datasets, particularly on 
medium-sized datasets (1,000+ samples). However, on the smallest dataset (303 samples), 
Logistic Regression outperformed both ensemble methods, suggesting that complex models overfit when 
data is very limited.

The Insurance dataset achieved the highest overall performance (ROC-AUC > 0.94) despite having only 
six features. This indicates that feature quality matters more than feature quantity for 
classification accuracy.

Based on these findings, we offer practical recommendations: for very small datasets 
(<500 samples), simpler linear models like Logistic Regression may be preferable; for medium-sized 
datasets (1,000-5,000 samples), Random Forest performs well with minimal hyperparameter tuning.

The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews related work on small dataset classification 
and tabular data methods. Section 3 describes our datasets and experimental methodology. Sections 4 
and 5 present results and discussion. Section 6 concludes with practical guidance for practitioners.

RELATED WORK

Over the past few years machine learning (ML) algorithm comparison research has been an area of 
growing interest among researchers. In their paper, Ahmed et al. [12] empirically evaluated ML 
models intending to compare algorithms utilized for time series forecasting; it was determined that 
there is not one algorithm that performs superbly outperforming all others across all datasets. 
Similarly, Pirooznia et al. [13] contrasted the performance of various methods that work with 
microarray data, discovering that both algorithm performance and rank differ with respect to 
datase characteristics. Uddin et al. [14] presented a comparison of various supervised learning 
algorithms designed for disease prediction, concluding that simpler approaches sometimes yield 
accuracy comparable to more complex algorithms, especially when data are limited.

Recent literature has also begun to target the unique challenges faced when working with small 
datasets. Based on their analysis of the performance of random forests on randomly sampled small 
datasets consisting of two-phase sampling, Han et al. [1] determined that new approaches need to be 
adopted if predictions created from these datasets are to avoid being overfit to the training data. 
In their work, Cha et al. [3] implemented a random forest-based predictive model trained using a 
small demolition waste database which had 784 samples and highlighted the need for thoroughly 
hyperparameter tuning of tree-based predictive models created from small sample datasets as a 
necessary requirement for accurate prediction in this instance.

Through their research, Probst et al. [2] contributed substantially to the understanding of random 
forest hyperparameters, providing guidance on how to appropriately select hyperparameters for use 
with random forests. Findings from their work indicate that in instances where datasets are small, 
hyperparameter settings that refer to the random forest's default hyperparameters produce the least 
amount of model accuracy. Furthermore, Probst and Boulesteix [4] focused on a specific hyperparameter setting, tree count for random forests, concluding that tree count does not detrimentally affect performance (i.e., predictive accuracy) but rather adds additional computation time without substantially improving performance relative to significantly increasing the time required to create the predictive model and all while utilizing small sample datasets.
For classification, tabular data continues to utilize logistic regression extensively; Tranmer & 
Elliot [5] conducted seminal research surrounding binary logistic regression used with survey data. 
Gilbert [6] published a study on tabular data through logistic regression that included a key 
strength of logistic models - easy interpretation as compared to "black box" types of models. 
Morgan & Teachman [7] provided comparisons presenting strong support to use logistic regression to 
classify structured data where a linearity assumption was reasonably made regarding relationships 
in data.

Many researchers have examined overfitting and ensemble methods. Kumar et al. [8] completed an 
exhaustive survey of ensemble methods and identified that overfitting was a major challenge with 
the small dataset sizes they identified within their research. Dietterich [9] contributed to the 
founding work on ensemble methods in 2000, where he emphasized the importance of base learner 
diversity for avoiding overfitting when training an ensemble. Pourtaheri & Zahiri [10] proposed 
improvements to techniques for reducing overfitting when price datasets are used. Cunningham [11]
studied ensemble classification schemes regarding overfitting; determining feature selection could 
help, but not entirely resolve the problem for very small datasets.

XGBoost has increased its presence as an applied machine learning method. Chen & Guestrin [16], 
introduced XGBoost with a reference implement that demonstrated the usefulness of XGBoost for 
classifying data on large scale datasets. Zhang et al. applied XGBoost to wind turbine fault 
detection where they were able to obtain a high degree of accuracy. However, the vast majority of 
studies conducted with respect to XGBoost focus on datasets that have thousands or millions of 
observations.
Comparison studies across several domains have not produced consistent findings; for example, 
Khondoker et al. used ML techniques on mental health data but observed no single ML method to be 
superior overall to others, while Onan et al. obtained positive results using ensemble methods 
within textual classification applications using data sets of much larger size than ours.

Also notable is that much of the previous research has either been performed using either 
large-scale data sets OR within a single-domain context, while there has been little to no 
work which has systematically compared performance between logistic regression, random forest, 
and XGBoost using small tabular data (<1500 samples) across multiple domains. This research fills 
an important gap in the current literature through the evaluation of all three techniques using 
four data sets with sample sizes ranging from 303 to 30,000, representing both the healthcare and 
finance industries.

3. METHODS

3.1 Datasets

We evaluated three classical machine learning models on four publicly available tabular datasets 
from healthcare and finance domains.

The Heart Disease dataset contains 303 patient records with 13 clinical features 
(age, sex, chest pain type, resting blood pressure, cholesterol, etc.) for predicting presence of 
heart disease. The dataset has balanced classes with approximately 50% positive cases.

The Credit Card Default dataset consists of 30,000 samples with 23 features describing customer 
payment history, credit limits, and demographics. The target variable indicates whether a customer 
defaulted on their credit card payment.

The German Credit dataset includes 999 applicants with 24 features describing financial status and 
credit history. The classification task is to predict credit risk (good or bad credit).

The Insurance Claims dataset contains 1,338 policyholders with 6 features including age, region, 
coverage type, and medical history. The target is binary claim occurrence.

3.2 Data Preprocessing

All datasets underwent identical preprocessing steps to ensure fair comparison. Missing values in 
numerical features were imputed using median values. Categorical features were encoded using label 
encoding, converting categories to integer representations. All features were then standardized 
using StandardScaler to have zero mean and unit variance.

3.3 Models

We compared three classical machine learning approaches:

Logistic Regression serves as our baseline linear model, using L2 regularization and the lbfgs 
solver with a maximum of 1000 iterations.

Random Forest is an ensemble method that builds 100 decision trees with bootstrap sampling. Each 
tree uses sqrt(n_features) random features at each split.

XGBoost is a gradient boosting implementation using 100 boosting rounds with a learning rate of 
0.1 and maximum tree depth of 3.

All models used default scikit-learn parameters except where specified, with random_state=42 for 
reproducibility.

3.4 Evaluation Protocol

Each dataset was split into 80% training and 20% testing sets using stratified sampling to preserve 
class distributions. Models were trained on the training set and evaluated on the held-out test set.

We report three metrics: Accuracy (overall correctness), F1 Score (harmonic mean of precision and 
recall), and ROC-AUC (area under the receiver operating characteristic curve). 
These metrics provide complementary views of model performance, especially for imbalanced datasets.

5. DISCUSSION

Random Forest achieved the best performance on three out of four datasets (Insurance, Credit Card, 
German Credit), demonstrating consistent effectiveness on medium-sized tabular datasets. On the 
Insurance dataset, Random Forest reached 94.4% accuracy compared to XGBoost's 93.3%, a meaningful 
difference for healthcare applications.

However, the Heart Disease dataset (303 samples) showed different behavior. Logistic Regression 
achieved 55.7% accuracy while Random Forest obtained only 52.5% and XGBoost 47.5%. We attribute 
this reversal to overfitting in ensemble methods when training data is extremely limited. With only 
303 samples, Random Forest and XGBoost likely fit noise in the training data rather than 
generalizable patterns. Logistic Regression, with its simpler decision boundary and fewer 
parameters, avoids this overfitting and generalizes better.

This finding aligns with bias-variance tradeoff theory. Ensemble methods have high variance and 
require sufficient training samples to estimate reliable tree splits. On very small datasets, 
the variance dominates, leading to poor generalization. Logistic Regression has higher bias 
but lower variance, making it more suitable for limited data.

The Credit Card dataset revealed the importance of examining multiple evaluation metrics. 
All three models achieved reasonable accuracy (80-81%) but poor F1-scores (0.36-0.46), 
indicating severe class imbalance. The models predicted the majority class frequently, 
inflating accuracy while failing to identify the minority class effectively. This demonstrates that 
accuracy alone is insufficient for imbalanced datasets.

Dataset size appears to be the critical factor determining model performance. Random Forest 
dominated on all datasets with 1,000+ samples, while Logistic Regression won on the dataset with 
fewer than 500 samples. This suggests a threshold around 500-800 samples where ensemble methods 
become beneficial, though additional datasets in this range would be needed to establish this 
threshold precisely.

Feature count does not necessarily correlate with performance. The Insurance dataset achieved the 
highest performance (94%+) with only 6 features, while German Credit with 24 features performed 
worse. This indicates that feature quality matters more than feature quantity. Feature selection 
\might improve German Credit performance by removing noisy variables.

Based on these findings, we recommend practitioners use Logistic Regression for datasets under 
500 samples, Random Forest for datasets between 1,000-5,000 samples, and consider XGBoost only when 
resources allow extensive hyperparameter tuning. When model interpretability is required for 
regulatory or clinical purposes, Logistic Regression remains preferable even with minor accuracy 
reduction.

Limitations of this study include: (1) only four datasets examined, all from healthcare and finance 
domains; (2) binary classification only; (3) no hyperparameter optimization performed; (4) no 
feature engineering attempted. Results may differ for multi-class problems, regression tasks, or 
datasets from other domains. We did not compare against recent deep learning methods designed for 
tabular data (e.g., TabNet, SAINT), which represents an area for future investigation.

The relatively low performance on Heart Disease (55.7% best accuracy) suggests either inherent 
dataset difficulty or missing domain knowledge that could inform better feature engineering. 
Future work should investigate whether domain-specific features improve performance.


6. CONCLUSION

This study compared Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost across four tabular datasets 
from healthcare and finance with sample sizes ranging from 303 to 30,000. Random Forest achieved 
the best performance on three out of four datasets, particularly on medium-sized datasets 
(1,000+ samples), reaching 94.4% accuracy on Insurance, 81.2% on Credit Card, and 76.5% on 
German Credit.

The key finding is that model selection depends critically on dataset size. On the smallest dataset 
(Heart Disease, 303 samples), Logistic Regression outperformed both ensemble methods 
(55.7% vs 52.5%), suggesting that simpler models generalize better when training data is severely 
limited. This pattern indicates a practical threshold around 500-800 samples below which ensemble 
methods may overfit.

Evaluation using multiple metrics proved essential. The Credit Card dataset showed reasonable 
accuracy (81%) but poor F1-scores (0.36-0.46), revealing severe class imbalance that accuracy alone 
would have missed.

For practitioners, we recommend: Logistic Regression for datasets under 500 samples; Random Forest 
for datasets between 1,000-5,000 samples with minimal tuning; and prioritizing interpretability 
over marginal accuracy gains when regulatory or clinical explanation is required.

Future work should examine additional datasets in the 200-800 sample range to establish more 
precise size thresholds, extend evaluation to multi-class and regression problems, compare against 
modern deep learning approaches for tabular data, and investigate whether domain-specific feature 
engineering can improve performance on difficult datasets like Heart Disease.
